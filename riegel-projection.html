Riegel Race-Time Projections

Deriving the endurance power law used to project times across distances

← Back to dashboard

1. The empirical observation

In 1977 Peter Riegel, analysing world-record performances across running, swimming and cycling, noticed that for a given athlete (or population) the relationship between race distance and time is almost perfectly a straight line on a log–log plot. A straight line in log–log space is, by definition, a power law:

$$ T \;=\; a\,D^{\,b} $$

where \(T\) is time, \(D\) is distance, and \(a, b\) are positive constants. The whole projection method is the consequence of this single fact.

2. From the power law to the projection formula

Suppose you have one known performance \((D_1, T_1)\) and want to predict the time \(T_2\) for a target distance \(D_2\). Both obey the same law:

$$ T_1 = a\,D_1^{\,b}, \qquad T_2 = a\,D_2^{\,b} $$

Dividing the second by the first cancels the athlete-specific constant \(a\):

$$ \frac{T_2}{T_1} \;=\; \frac{a\,D_2^{\,b}}{a\,D_1^{\,b}} \;=\; \left(\frac{D_2}{D_1}\right)^{\!b} $$

which rearranges to the Riegel projection formula:

$$ \boxed{\; T_2 \;=\; T_1 \left(\frac{D_2}{D_1}\right)^{\!c} \;}\qquad c \equiv b $$

The exponent \(c\) is Riegel's fatigue factor. Only the ratio of distances matters — the constant \(a\) (your absolute speed) never has to be known, which is what makes a single piece enough to project from.

3. Why \(c \approx 1.06\), and what it means

Consider the average speed implied by the law, \(v = D/T\):

$$ v \;=\; \frac{D}{T} \;=\; \frac{D}{a\,D^{\,b}} \;=\; \frac{1}{a}\,D^{\,1-b} $$

If you could hold the same speed at every distance, \(v\) would be constant, forcing \(b = 1\) (time exactly proportional to distance). Real athletes slow down as distance grows, so \(v\) must decrease with \(D\), which requires the exponent \(1-b < 0\), i.e. \(b = c > 1\). Riegel's regressions put that value very consistently near:

$$ c \;\approx\; 1.06 $$

The excess over 1 is the fatigue penalty. Each time you double the distance, the time more than doubles by a factor \(2^{\,c}\):

$$ \frac{T(2D)}{T(D)} \;=\; 2^{c} \;=\; 2^{1.06} \;\approx\; 2.085 $$

so doubling the distance costs about 8.5% more than double the time — equivalently, average speed drops by a factor \(2^{\,1-c}=2^{-0.06}\approx 0.96\), i.e. roughly 4% slower per doubling. (This is the continuous cousin of rowing's rule-of-thumb "Paul's Law": add ~5 s/500m for each doubling of distance.)

4. Projecting a distance from a time target

For fixed-time targets (e.g. a 30-minute test) we invert the formula. Starting again from \(T = a D^{c}\) and solving for \(D\):

$$ D \;=\; \left(\frac{T}{a}\right)^{1/c} $$

Taking the ratio of a target \((D_2, T_2)\) to the known \((D_1, T_1)\):

$$ \boxed{\; D_2 \;=\; D_1 \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^{1/c} \;} $$

The reciprocal exponent \(1/c \approx 0.943\) is simply the original power law read the other way: \(b\) maps distance→time, \(1/b\) maps time→distance.

5. Converting to pace

Every projection is reported as a /500 m split, the rowing convention. Pace is just time scaled to a 500 m unit:

$$ \text{pace}_{500} \;=\; \frac{T_2}{D_2}\times 500 \quad\left[\tfrac{\text{s}}{500\text{m}}\right] $$

and is then formatted as m:ss.s.

6. Worked example

From your 12 710 m row in \(T_1 = 3187.2\,\text{s}\) (53:07.2), with \(c = 1.06\):

Project a 2 000 m time

$$ T_2 = 3187.2\left(\frac{2000}{12710}\right)^{1.06} = 3187.2 \times (0.1574)^{1.06} = 448.9\,\text{s} = 7\!:\!28.9 $$ $$ \text{pace} = \frac{448.9}{2000}\times 500 = 112.2\,\text{s} = 1\!:\!52.2/500\text{m} $$

7. Assumptions & limitations

  • Single global exponent. \(c = 1.06\) is a population average. A strong aerobic athlete may run a flatter curve (\(c\) closer to 1); a sprint-type athlete a steeper one. The projection is only as good as that assumption for you.
  • Don't extrapolate wildly. Riegel himself bounded the model to roughly the 3.5-minute to 4-hour range. Projecting a marathon from a 500 m sprint (or vice-versa) leaves the law's validity window.
  • Source quality matters. The projection inherits the pacing of the piece you feed it: a poorly paced or interrupted row projects poorly.
  • Erg ≠ water. These are ergometer projections; on-water times add boat, crew, and conditions effects the model knows nothing about.
Best practice: project from a piece of similar duration to the target. A 2k estimate is most trustworthy when derived from a 5–20 min effort, not from an hour-long steady row.

8. Reference

Riegel, P. S. (1981). “Athletic Records and Human Endurance.” American Scientist, 69(3), 285–290. The original formulation \(T = a D^{1.06}\) and the term “endurance exponent.”
