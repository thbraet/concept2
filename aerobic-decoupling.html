Aerobic Decoupling (Pw:Hr)

How the dashboard computes cardiovascular drift over a piece

← Back to dashboard

1. What it measures

During a steady-state effort, your power output should track your heart rate. As a piece wears on, cardiac drift sets in: heart rate creeps upward even though power stays constant (driven by rising core temperature, dehydration, and falling stroke volume). Aerobic decoupling quantifies that drift as the percentage decline in the efficiency factor (output per heartbeat) between the first and second halves of the workout.

It is a standard endurance-durability marker, popularised by Joe Friel and TrainingPeaks as Pw:Hr (power-to-heart-rate). A small decoupling means your aerobic system held the output cheaply to the end; a large one means it became progressively more expensive.

2. The efficiency factor

For a single stroke \(i\) we have an instantaneous power \(P_i\) (watts) and a heart rate \(\text{HR}_i\) (bpm). The per-stroke efficiency factor is their ratio:

$$ \mathrm{EF}_i \;=\; \frac{P_i}{\text{HR}_i} \qquad \left[\frac{\text{W}}{\text{bpm}}\right] $$

Power is not stored per stroke by the erg, so it is reconstructed from the recorded pace \(p_i\) (seconds per 500 m) using Concept2's published relationship between pace and power:

$$ P_i \;=\; \frac{2.80}{v_i^{\,3}}, \qquad v_i \;=\; \frac{p_i}{500} \;\;\left[\frac{\text{s}}{\text{m}}\right] $$

where \(v_i\) is the per-metre split (seconds per metre) and the constant \(2.80\) is the drag coefficient baked into the PM5's power model. The cube law is the key physical fact: erg resistance is dominated by the flywheel's air drag, for which power scales with the cube of speed.

3. Splitting the piece

Let the workout span elapsed time \(t \in [0, T]\). We bisect it at the time midpoint \(t_{\text{mid}} = T/2\) into a first half \(\mathcal{H}_1 = \{\, i : t_i \le t_{\text{mid}} \,\}\) and a second half \(\mathcal{H}_2 = \{\, i : t_i > t_{\text{mid}} \,\}\).

The efficiency factor of each half is the mean of its per-stroke ratios (strokes with a missing or zero heart rate are excluded):

$$ \overline{\mathrm{EF}}_h \;=\; \frac{1}{\lvert \mathcal{H}_h \rvert} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{H}_h} \frac{P_i}{\text{HR}_i}, \qquad h \in \{1, 2\} $$
Note we average the ratios \(P_i/\text{HR}_i\), not the ratio of the averages \(\overline{P}/\overline{\text{HR}}\). For steady pieces the two agree closely; averaging the ratios is more robust to the brief power spikes the cube law produces from small pace fluctuations.

4. The decoupling figure

Decoupling is the relative drop in efficiency from the first half to the second:

$$ \boxed{\;D \;=\; \frac{\overline{\mathrm{EF}}_1 - \overline{\mathrm{EF}}_2}{\overline{\mathrm{EF}}_1} \times 100\%\;} $$
  • \(D > 0\) — efficiency fell. Either heart rate drifted up at the same power, or power sagged at the same heart rate. This is the usual, expected direction; its size measures fatigue/durability.
  • \(D \approx 0\) — power and heart rate stayed locked together; a well-paced aerobic effort.
  • \(D < 0\) — efficiency improved in the second half, typically a negative split or a too-conservative start (a warm-up artefact as HR lags the early effort).

Interpreting the magnitude (Friel)

DecouplingReading
< 5%Strong aerobic durability — coupled to the end
5–8%Acceptable for a long/hard piece
> 8%Aerobic base or pacing is the limiter; effort faded
Thresholds assume a genuine steady-state piece in roughly zone 2. Intervals, sprints, or pyramids will read high by construction — the metric is only meaningful for continuous efforts.

5. Worked example

Your 12 710 m / 53:07 row (id 117417773), bisected at \(t_{\text{mid}} = 1593.6\,\text{s}\):

$$ \overline{\mathrm{EF}}_1 = 1.577\ \tfrac{\text{W}}{\text{bpm}}, \qquad \overline{\mathrm{EF}}_2 = 1.430\ \tfrac{\text{W}}{\text{bpm}} $$ $$ D = \frac{1.577 - 1.430}{1.577}\times 100\% \;=\; 9.3\% $$

A decoupling of 9.3% sits above the 5% durability line: across this long steady row, holding the pace cost progressively more heartbeats toward the end — a signal that the aerobic base (or the day's freshness) was the limiter rather than the legs.

6. Assumptions & limitations

  • Power is modelled from pace via the cube law, not measured directly; brief pace noise is amplified, which the ratio-averaging in §3 damps.
  • A clean heart-rate trace is required. Dropouts are skipped; a glitchy monitor will distort the figure (the dashboard already excludes known-bad HR workouts).
  • The half-split is by time, not distance — appropriate since drift is a time-dependent physiological process.
  • Only valid for continuous, steady efforts; see the warning in §4.
